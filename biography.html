Early Life and Education

Magnús Pálsson was born in Eskifjörður, Iceland, in 1929 and moved with his family to the Reykjavík area a few years later. As a child, he drew extensively, as children do, but simply continued doing so throughout his life. Some pictures found their way onto walls and remain there still, like his painting of Írufoss in Grímsnes before it was dammed. The family had a retreat in that area during the summers, during the war—World War II, which had considerable impact on Iceland. The country was occupied by the British, fishing boats and cargo ships were shot down by Nazi submarines, and the death toll was proportionally no less than in Europe.

At the war's end, around the time British soldiers stopped marching through Reykjavík's streets, Magnús moved downtown with his family and entered the art department of Reykjavík Grammar School. He was sociable and became chairman of the Christmas committee, organizing dances and theatrical performances while decorating the school extensively with enormous colored drawings inspired by mythology and aesthetics of ancient Greece or Egypt. He also joined the drama committee and participated in the school's public performances at Iðnó theater, alongside Anna Sigríður Gunnarsdóttir, his future first wife. He gained acclaim for his role as the son in the comedy "Hay Fever" (1948) by Noël Coward, directed by Lárus Sigurbjörnsson, in the spring he graduated.

When Magnús left for studies in the autumn, he might have followed family tradition by studying theology or law, but his mind was clear about what he wanted—he chose theater and visual arts. He went to Birmingham from 1948-50. That city, then Britain's second largest, was an old cultural and commercial center that had suffered considerable destruction from bombing raids but had also produced about ten thousand Spitfire MK II aircraft in its own factories. In 1949, BBC began its first television broadcasts outside London there, though it would be long before Icelanders could watch their own television station.

Magnús studied at the newly established Crescent Theater School of Design in stage design. He had an excellent, experienced professor, was enthusiastic, and the time served him well. He frequently attended cinema and theater, including Birmingham Rep and the Royal Shakespeare Company in Stratford-upon-Avon, which performed works by Shakespeare alongside authors like John Osborne, Terence Rattigan, and Samuel Beckett. He often traveled to London for performances and to visit museums that were gradually reopening after moving their collections to safety during the war. There was an upsurge in the arts as the public wanted to recover and receive artistic nourishment after the deprivation of the war years.

Theater Career

When Magnús returned to Iceland, he worked as property master at the newly established National Theatre while simultaneously pursuing additional education in visual arts. He and Anna Sigríður Gunnarsdóttir had their first child, Páll, in 1952, followed by Tumi in 1957, Pétur in 1958, Guttormur in 1960, and Anna Sigurveig in 1967. During his first five theater seasons, Magnús created stage designs for about twelve productions, more often for Reykjavík Theatre Company at Iðnó than for the main stage of the National Theatre.

From the beginning, Magnús's stage designs bore a strong resemblance to what would later characterize his work in theater: "drawing" and spaces with simple forms. His hallmark was also evident—the ability to play with and see everyday objects in ways that few others could, and to display them accordingly. His work was characterized by strong visual concepts and a distinctive personal aesthetic that challenged conventional theatrical presentation.

Over his career, Magnús worked extensively in theater for decades, creating approximately 80 stage designs for professional theaters and numerous amateur theaters across Iceland over a 46-year period from 1950. He played a major role in founding the experimental theater group Gríma around 1960, which was instrumental in introducing contemporary European and American avant-garde works to Icelandic audiences. Through Gríma, he helped bring works by Sartre, Beckett, and other avant-garde playwrights to Icelandic stages, fundamentally changing the landscape of Icelandic theater.

Transition to Visual Arts

In the late 1950s and early 1960s, Magnús began to engage more deeply with the visual arts, working alongside his close friend and collaborator, the Swiss-German graphic designer Dieter Roth. Together, they are rightly credited as the pioneers of conceptual art and Fluxus in Iceland, having laid the foundations for the varied and flourishing arts scene that Iceland enjoys today.

Magnús opened his first private exhibition in September 1968 with an eclectic assembly of sculptures, drawings and found objects. The exhibition received favorable reviews from knowledgeable critics but confused the general public, who were only just getting used to abstract paintings. This eclecticism has characterized his art ever since, along with his total disregard for the boundaries between different genres of art.

Artistic Practice

His works span sculpture, performance, book works, plays, voice sculptures, and experimental music. He has consistently sought to break down walls between art forms and redefine them, resulting in works that blend all elements: sculpture, drawing, writing, performance, video, music, theater, and even teaching—the last of which he acknowledges to be the trickiest art of them all.

Magnús was a key player in the great changes that occurred in visual art in the sixties and seventies. Revising the nature of artistic creation, he helped bring about a new imagery based on a critical stance towards art history, while simultaneously opening new paths to the future. His works, while sharing some spirit with Fluxus, pop and conceptual art movements, have proven to be quite unique.

Music and Sound Art

Through his collaboration with Dieter Roth, Magnús became involved with the FLUXUS movement. In 1984, when leaving his position at the Iceland Academy of the Arts, he released a cassette titled "Teaching: The Craziest Art Form" in connection with an art exhibition during the Reykjavík Arts Festival. His sound works can be found on various albums released by the Fluxus movement and other art-related record releases.

Teaching and Institutional Contributions

In 1975, Hildur Hákonardóttir, then principal of the Iceland Academy of the Arts and Crafts, invited Magnús to establish and lead the Experimental Art Department (Nýlistadeild) within the school. He served there until 1984, during which time many of Iceland's most renowned artists graduated under his guidance. The department and his teaching methods were controversial among older visual artists, as they emphasized freedom in both attendance and artistic creation. The band Bruni BB notably operated within the experimental department during this period.

As a teacher, Magnús has had an immense effect on generations of artists. He taught widely at art schools abroad and considers teaching itself as an art form. His influence is widely visible in Icelandic art life, and it's no exaggeration to say that the distinctive character of Icelandic visual art stems in part from him.

In 1987, Magnús moved to London with his second wife, Dr. Frances Cowan, where they had a son, Kolbeinn, in 1990. They lived in London until 2008, when they relocated to Iceland. Throughout this period, Magnús continued his artistic practice while engaging with the Fluxus community and developing his sound poetry and performance work.

International Recognition and Initiatives

Magnús represented Iceland at the Venice Biennale in 1980. He founded and directed the international workshop "Mob Shop" (1981-1989), established the performance choir Nýlókórinn, was one of the founding members of the Living Art Museum (Nýlistasafnið) in 1978, co-founded Hong Kong Press publishing house in Gothenburg in 1986, and established the Experimental Art Department at the Iceland Academy of the Arts and Crafts.

Museum Collections and Archives

In 2013, the Reykjavík Art Museum focused on Magnús Pálsson's performances from 1980 to 2013 in the exhibition "The Sound of a Bugle in a Shoebox," produced in collaboration with the Reykjavík Arts Festival. In 2019, the retrospective exhibition "SOMETHING from NOTHING" focused on the visual realm found in physical works by the artist, with selected pieces from the early sixties to today reflecting the fertile ways of thinking that Pálsson has consistently demonstrated in sculptures, book works, and two-dimensional works.

The Reykjavík Art Museum preserves the Magnús Pálsson Archive, which the artist has built over many decades. Preserved in approximately three hundred boxes, the material provides an invaluable insight into Pálsson's artistic creation.

Recognition and Legacy

Magnús has received numerous awards for his life's work, including being named Reykjavík City Artist in 2011. His career has been remarkable for the breadth of his activities and his influence. As a performance artist, Pálsson is unquestionably a pioneer, and his contributions have fundamentally shaped the development of contemporary art in Iceland.
